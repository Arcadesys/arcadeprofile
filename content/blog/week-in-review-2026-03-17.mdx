# The Weekend I Let the Machines Cook

I have a ten-agent team running inside my laptop. This weekend, I pointed them at everything I'd been putting off and said *go*.

Here's what happened.

---

## Three Novels Got Their Editorial Pass

This is the one that matters most to me.

I've been sitting on manuscripts for years. Not because they weren't written — they were. Rough drafts, complete, gathering dust in folders because the revision process was a wall I couldn't climb. Low vision, neurodivergent, and deeply allergic to the repetitive visual slog of traditional editing. The books existed. The polished versions didn't.

This weekend, that changed for three of them at once.

**The Two-Flat Cats** — my queer speculative novel about toon identity and civil rights — got a full editorial pass. Line editing across all 85 chapters. A structural review of the sections I'd been worried about. A pronoun audit to make sure Ky's transition from they/them to he/him lands at exactly the right chapter. And then it got prepped for beta readers, which means it's actually going out to human eyes now. That's real.

**Bait and Switch** got its revised edition treatment. Sensitivity review, structural and pacing review, line edits, continuity and worldbuilding audit. This book has been out in the world since 2010, but the new edition is sharper. The civil rights allegory hits harder when the prose isn't getting in its own way.

**The Painted Cat** went through the same process. Full edit pass, worldbuilding insertions to deepen the Ink mythology.

And then — because why stop at individual books — the editorial team ran a cross-trilogy worldbuilding pass. All three novels share the same universe, and I'd never done a proper consistency audit across them. Now I have one. Timeline reconciliation, worldbuilding insertions threaded through all three books, sensitivity reviews on every new passage.

Three manuscripts that had been waiting years. Done in a weekend. Not because a machine wrote them — because a machine finally made editing *accessible* for me.

## I Built an Interactive Music Toy

Sometimes you need to make something that doesn't matter.

Balldrop started as "what if you could drop balls onto beams and they made music?" That's it. That's the whole pitch. Physics engine, synthesizer, neon aesthetic. You draw beams with your mouse, drop balls from the top of the screen, and they bounce off the beams and play notes based on where they hit.

It went from zero to fully playable in a single day. Matter.js for physics, Tone.js for audio, Canvas for rendering, neon glow effects because I wanted it to look like a Tron arcade cabinet. There's a demo scene that plays itself when you first load it. There are keyboard shortcuts. There's a ball dispenser.

It is, objectively, a toy. But it's *my* toy, and building it in a day felt like proof of concept for something larger — that small, delightful, interactive things can exist on the internet without needing to be products.

## The Profile Site Got Overhauled

The site you're reading this on didn't look like this last Thursday.

It was a mess — leftover build artifacts in git, no consolidated data source for my books, a professional page that didn't reflect who I actually am. Over the weekend it got a proper redesign: cleaned-up codebase, consolidated book data, a portfolio page, a demos section for hosting interactive experiments (hello, Balldrop), and this blog you're reading right now, running on an MDX engine with RSS.

The sidebar in my writing tools got reworked too. Scene cards are compact one-liners now instead of sprawling panels. The manuscript view and outline got consolidated into a single pane. Small things that make the daily work of writing less friction.

## I Conceived a Product and Killed It in 48 Hours

This is maybe the most honest thing I can tell you about how I work.

Saturday morning I had an idea: Magic Mirror. A photobooth for furry conventions that uses AI image generation to transform your selfie into your fursona. Species picker, customization panel, Gemini image pipeline, QR code delivery, kiosk mode, Square payment integration. The whole thing.

By Sunday night, it was built. Working prototype. Multiple model adapters. Benchmark suite. QA automation. Kiosk app ready to deploy.

By Monday, I'd killed it.

There were already comparable products on the market. And the specific artistic vision I had — the particular aesthetic I wanted, the kind of transformation that would feel *right* rather than just technically impressive — required a specific crowd. A convention photobooth aimed at general attendees wasn't going to be the right venue for it.

So I shelved it. Forty-eight hours of work, parked. And I'm fine with that, because the alternative is worse: spending months polishing something for the wrong audience. The speed of the build is what made the kill painless. When it only costs a weekend to find out an idea doesn't fit, you can afford to be honest about it.

---

## The Meta Layer

Here's what I actually learned this weekend: the bottleneck was never capability. It was coordination.

I've had the manuscripts. I've had the ideas. What I didn't have was a way to move on all of them simultaneously without burning out. Ten agents working in parallel — editorial team on the novels, engineering team on the site and the toys, product team on Magic Mirror — meant I could make progress on everything at once while making decisions only on the things that needed my judgment.

The agents don't replace the creative work. They replace the *administrative overhead* of creative work. The scheduling, the tracking, the "which thing do I do next" paralysis that kills momentum for neurodivergent brains.

Ninety tasks completed since Friday. Three manuscripts through editorial. A toy built. A site overhauled. A product conceived and killed. Not bad for a weekend.
