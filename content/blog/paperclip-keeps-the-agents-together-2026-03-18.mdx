# Paperclip Keeps the Agents Together

I got interested in Paperclip after trying OpenClaw and finding it didn't quite click for me. Looking back, I think the problem was that I never knew what the agent was going to *do*. I'd spend time digging through the guts of the thing, agent by agent, hoping to untangle the mess I'd made. Every session felt like archaeology. I wasn't building — I was excavating.

But then a friend showed Paperclip in our AI channel. I downloaded and installed it for a one-night tinker.

It's now day five, and by my dashboard's accounting, I've burned a respectable chunk of Anthropic's money on their max plan for a month.

I'm not mad about it.

---

## The Company Metaphor

Paperclip flips the usual agent orchestration model on its head. Instead of hiding which agent does what, it leans into a company metaphor. You talk to the CEO for strategy. The editorial team for refinement. The CMO for positioning. You always know who you're talking to, and that clarity makes the system feel grounded, legible, and trustworthy.

This matters more than it sounds like it should. Most multi-agent setups treat agents like interchangeable black boxes — you throw a prompt in and hope the right thing comes back. Paperclip gives each agent a name, a role, and a chain of command. When my editorial agent sends work to the line editor, I can see it happen. When the CMO drafts positioning, I know where to push back. There's no mystery about who holds the context for what.

It turns out that when you can see the org chart, you stop fighting the system and start *using* it.

---

## The Kanban Backbone

What makes it click is the Kanban backbone. It looks and behaves like any product tool you've used before: tickets, assignments, projects, goals that feed downstream work. There's no real onboarding. You start moving.

I push a chunk of work into the system, then queue up the next thing while I review what came back. It feels a little unhinged. It is also the fastest I have ever moved. The rhythm is addictive — file a task, context-switch to something creative, come back twenty minutes later to a draft or a build or a review. My brain gets to do the fun parts while the boring parts happen in the background.

For someone who's neurodivergent, this is not a small thing. The "which thing do I do next" paralysis that usually eats my mornings just... stopped. The board tells me what's in progress, what's waiting, and what's done. I don't have to hold the state in my head anymore. The system holds it for me.

---

## Routing on Purpose

And you route on purpose. One agent doesn't have to hold the whole problem. Need a graphic? I call Fresco, my artist agent, wired into image tools. Need a blog post shaped from a Discord rant? The content writer handles it. Need the prose tightened? The editor picks it up. Each agent has a lane, and the lanes connect.

The loop I use most is simple: I walk around the house talking through ideas, draft a rough spec on my phone, tighten it at my desk, and hand it to the Paperclip CEO. About an hour later, I have something real to test. The CEO breaks it into subtasks, assigns the right agents, and the work fans out. I review what comes back, push notes, and the cycle starts again.

The loop just flows. I keep finding reasons to run it again.

---

## The Honest Take

Paperclip is 100% worth it. If you've been looking for a way to actually orchestrate agents instead of babysitting them — to move faster without losing track of what's happening — this is the tool that made it click for me. Five days in and I've shipped more than I did in the previous month.

But fair warning: it's a real quarter muncher. You will burn through tokens. Budget accordingly, watch your dashboard, and don't be surprised when the bill shows up. The speed is real. So is the meter.
