# I Revised Three Novels Last Week. Here's What Actually Happened.

I revised three novels last week.

Not "asked AI to rewrite them." Revised. The tedious, specific, editorial work of making manuscripts better. The kind of work I'd been avoiding for years because it's visually intensive and I'm legally blind and my brain would rather write a new book than fix an old one.

Here's what happened.

---

I have a multi-agent editorial pipeline. Not one AI doing everything badly — specialized agents, each focused on a different dimension of craft. Structural editor. Line editor. Continuity checker. Sensitivity reader. Editor-in-chief who synthesizes the whole mess into a coherent editorial direction.

They don't write. They read, flag, and suggest. I decide.

Last week I pushed three manuscripts through the pipeline: *The Two-Flat Cats* (queer speculative, 70K words), the revised *Bait and Switch* (YA civil rights allegory), and *Single Player Co-Op* (memoir about DID, blindness, and survival).

The structural editor caught that chapter 12 was doing setup work chapter 10 already handled. The continuity editor found a timeline contradiction I'd missed for three years. The line editor tightened sentences in exactly the way I would have, given another six hours of staring at the page.

Three manuscripts that had been sitting in folders. Moving again.

---

The conversation about AI in creative work is stuck on a binary: replacement vs. magic wand. What I'm doing is neither.

I built editorial infrastructure. The same way a recording studio is infrastructure for a musician. The creative act happens inside the infrastructure, not because of it. Every story starts in my head. Every final word is my decision. The agents find the problems. I fix them.

Publishing houses have always worked this way — different editors bringing different expertise to the same manuscript. I just built one that fits in my laptop and doesn't require me to see the page clearly to use it.

I'm not hiding the machinery. I'm showing it to you. Because I think there are other writers out there — disabled writers, neurodivergent writers, writers with a drawer full of unfinished work — who might look at this and think: *oh. That's possible.*

It is. I did it last week.
