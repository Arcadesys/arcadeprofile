# The Social Singularity

*Part one of a series.*

---

There is a Telegram chat I visit most days. It is full of MAGA channers.

They post Pepe edits. They argue about tariffs. Some of them have opinions about demographics that would get them banned from most platforms. The political range runs from libertarian to "I think the government should be smaller" to things I will not repeat here.

They are also, at this moment, my furry art community.

I need you to sit with that for a second.

These are the people I share my art with. My furry art. The character designs I've spent months refining, the illustrations I've built pipeline by pipeline, the work I pour genuine craft into. I show it to the MAGA channers. They give me feedback. They riff on it. They share their own work. The chat is alive and generative and weird in the way that good creative communities are weird.

And the reason I am there, instead of with my own people, is that my own people made it impossible to show up.

---

## How We Got Here

I am a stubborn person. I have trouble keeping my mouth shut.

This is not new information for anyone who knows me. I've been this way my entire life — the kid who asks the teacher why, the adult who says the thing everyone is thinking but nobody wants to say out loud. It's a feature. It's also, sometimes, a bug.

The AI conversation in the furry community required silence. Not the explicit kind — nobody sent me a letter saying "shut up or else." The implicit kind. The kind where you watch the temperature of a room and you know, with the social fluency that comes from decades of navigating queer spaces, that the correct move is to nod and change the subject.

I did not nod. I did not change the subject.

I talked about the tools I was building. I shared the art I was making. I said, out loud, that these tools were going to matter and that ignoring them was a mistake. I said it with enthusiasm, because I was enthusiastic, and because I have never been good at performing feelings I don't have.

The community didn't kick me out. That's not how it works. What happened was quieter and worse. The DMs slowed down. The group chats moved without me. The art I posted got no engagement — not criticism, not curiosity, just silence. The kind of silence that tells you the door is closed and nobody is going to say so.

So I armored up. I hate that. I hate it.

---

## The Armor

I go to meetups now. I see people I've known for years. I hug them. I ask about their projects. And when they ask about mine, I edit.

I don't talk about the editorial engine I built that lets me finish novels. I don't talk about the agent coordination system or the art pipelines or the fact that I've spent two years learning to do things that will matter in five. I talk about the safe parts. The parts that won't trigger the silence.

This is what armor does. It keeps you safe. It also keeps you alone inside it.

The friendships that survived are real. I want to be clear about that. I have genuine friends from this community who stuck around, who ask honest questions, who don't agree with me but don't need me to be someone else either. I am lucky to have them.

But I approach the space different now. There is a calculation running in the back of my head that was not there before — what can I say, what can I share, how much of myself is safe to bring into this room. I never used to think about that. The furry community was the one place I didn't have to think about that.

I wonder, sometimes, how much of the old closeness was real. How much was the ease of agreement masquerading as intimacy. I don't have an answer. I just have the question, and it sits in my chest like a stone.

---

## The Math

Here is the part where I am not emotional. Here is the part where I am just looking at numbers.

Two years ago, AI art tools were nightmarish betas. The hands were wrong. The outputs were uncanny. Building anything with them required technical knowledge, command-line fluency, and a tolerance for garbage output. The barrier to entry was high. The results were bad. The skepticism was reasonable.

That was two years ago.

Today, you can build a production-ready art pipeline through a GUI. You can train a model on your own style in an afternoon. The hands are mostly right. The outputs are good enough that the distinction between "AI-assisted" and "traditional" is becoming a question of process, not quality.

The cost of learning this technology right now — while it is young, while the community of practice is small, while the tools are still simple enough to understand — is low. An afternoon here. A weekend there. Curiosity and a willingness to be bad at something new.

The cost of catching up later, when the tools are mature and complex and the people who learned early have a two-year head start — that cost is high. That cost is the kind of cost that makes people give up before they start.

This is not an argument. I am not trying to convince anyone. It is just the math. The community is refusing to pay the small cost now. They will pay the large cost later. Or they won't pay it at all, and they'll watch the space they care about get shaped by people who don't share their values.

Which brings me to the part that hurts.

---

## The Red Pants

Someone will read this and ask: why are you using the same tool they are?

"They" meaning the content farms. The slop merchants. The people who use AI to generate ten thousand pieces of garbage and flood every platform with noise. Why would you, a person who cares about craft, use the same technology that is being used to destroy craft?

The answer is the French army in 1914.

They marched into World War One wearing bright red pants. They did bayonet charges across open fields. They did this because that is what soldiers looked like. That is what war looked like. They had tradition and élan and a very clear idea of how fighting was supposed to work.

The Germans had machine guns.

The French lost 27,000 soldiers in a single day. August 22, 1914. The deadliest day in French military history. Not because they were cowards. Not because they lacked conviction. Because they brought principle to a fight that had already changed, and principle does not stop bullets.

Tradition and conviction do not stop an exponential curve. Standing on principle while the curve keeps climbing is just a stylish way to lose.

I don't want to lose. And I don't want my community to lose either. But you can't win a fight you refuse to show up for.

---

## The Vacancy

Here is the political point, and I will make it once.

Refusing to engage with AI does not make AI go away. The technology does not care about your position on it. The curve does not flatten because you find it distasteful.

What happens when people who care about values and community and craft step back from a technology is simple: they leave a vacancy. And vacancies get filled.

They get filled by people who do not care about values. Who do not care about community. Who do not care about craft. They get filled by the content farms and the slop merchants and the people who see this technology as a way to extract value, not create it.

I am already watching this happen. I am already living it. The Telegram chat full of MAGA channers? They are filling the vacancy. They are building with these tools, riffing with these tools, creating community around these tools. And they are doing it because the people who should be doing it — the people who care about the things I care about — decided that engagement was collaboration and silence was resistance.

Silence is not resistance. Silence is abdication. And the chair doesn't stay empty.

---

## The Stakes

I want to be careful here. I don't want to oversell the danger, because I've spent this entire essay trying to be honest and overselling would be dishonest.

But I also can't undersell it.

We are looking at what the researchers call a [hard takeoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technological_singularity#Hard_vs._soft_takeoff) — the point where AI capability improvement becomes self-reinforcing and the rate of change exceeds our ability to adapt to it. Whether we're there yet is debatable. That we're closer than most people think is not.

We have a government that will not steward us through this moment. That is not a political statement. It is an observation. The regulatory infrastructure does not exist. The political will does not exist. The institutional knowledge does not exist. We are on our own.

This is not a minor cultural spat about art styles and community norms. This is the ground shifting under every assumption we've built our creative lives on — who makes things, how they make them, what counts, who decides. And the people who care the most are the ones stepping back the fastest.

I don't have a clean ending for this. I don't have a call to action or a rallying cry or a five-point plan for fixing the fracture. I have a Telegram chat full of channers who like my furry art. I have friendships wrapped in armor. I have a front-row seat to an exponential curve that doesn't care what any of us think about it.

Live or die, man. Live or die.
